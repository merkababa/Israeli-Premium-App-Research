## 2. Government Policy Changes 2025-2026

### Subsidy Structure (Current)
- **Tax deduction:** NIS 940/month ($263) per child under 3 if both parents work full-time
- **Kindergarten subsidies:** Up to NIS 1,700/month in low-income towns; NIS 200-300/month in the center
- **Income-based daycare:** Low-income families pay NIS 150-300/month; middle-income on sliding scale
- **Eligibility requirement:** Both parents must work 24+ hours/week for subsidized rates

### Major Policy Development: Early Childhood Authority
- **Knesset is pushing to create a new national Early Childhood Authority** under the Education Ministry — the first centralized body for children 0-3
- Will manage all licensing, supervision, subsidies, and professional policy for daycare centers
- Triggered by the Jerusalem daycare deaths (January 2026)

### Budget Concerns
- **Supervision funding DROPPING:** NIS 46 million allocated for 2026, down from NIS 190 million in 2023 (a 76% cut)
- Only 59 inspectors + 13 advisers for the entire country
- Current enforcement capacity is severely inadequate

### Haredi Daycare Subsidies
- Attorney General cancelled daycare subsidies for fathers who don't serve in the army (July 2024, following High Court ruling)
- This created acute pressure in Haredi communities, pushing more children into unlicensed care

### Key Takeaway
Government is trying to increase oversight of formal daycare but has CUT the budget to do it. The subsidy structure pushes dual-income families toward formal daycare, but supply is insufficient. This gap between policy ambition and execution creates demand for flexible alternatives — Emuni's sweet spot.

## 3. Post-War (Oct 7) Impact on Childcare Demand

### Displacement and Emergency Childcare
- Since Oct 8, 2023: **Dror Israel operated emergency programs in 60+ hotels** for evacuees, including:
  - 18 preschools for ages 0-6
  - Drop-in childcare in 50+ locations
  - After-school programs in 30 evacuee centers
  - Served 41,000+ participants
- Northern border evacuees (Kiryat Shmona, etc.) relocated across the country — children experienced school dropouts and behavioral challenges
- Childcare costs were covered for hospital workers and essential staff during school closures

### Caregiver Supply Shock
- **Foreign caregiver shortage post-Oct 7:** Recruitment of foreign caregivers (primarily Filipino) became "increasingly difficult" since the war
- Filipino caregivers account for 36% of all permitted migrant care workers (53% of all migrant workers with permits are in caregiving)
- ~30,000 Filipinos work in Israel (caregivers + hotel staff); many were relocated from high-risk areas or repatriated
- The war created a **supply-side shock** to available caregivers

### Economic Impact on Families
- Extensive military reserve mobilization disrupted family routines
- Q2 2025 saw a 4.3% GDP contraction due to war with Iran and reserve mobilization — families needed more childcare flexibility during these disruptions

### Key Takeaway
The war created both supply disruption (fewer foreign caregivers available) and demand surges (displaced families, reserve duty gaps, school closures). This is a tailwind for Emuni: families need flexible, on-demand babysitting more than ever, and the traditional supply channels are strained.

## 4. Birth Rate & Demographic Trends

### Current Fertility Data
- **TFR (2024): 2.85 children per woman** — well above replacement level of 2.1
- Birth rate (2024): 18.77 per 1,000 (0.9% increase from 2023)
- Israel remains the **highest-fertility OECD country** by a wide margin

### Demographic Shift Underway
- **Population growth dropped below 1% for the first time** in Israel's history (0.9% in 2025, per Taub Center)
- **Negative net migration** expected to continue: gap projected at 37,000 in 2025, continuing into 2026 — unprecedented in Israel's history
- Secular/traditional women's completed fertility trending toward **1.7 children** by late 2030s
- Religious women trending toward **2.3 children**
- Haredi women trending toward **4.3 children** (down from higher historical rates)

### What This Means
- The total market of children needing care is still growing (TFR 2.85 is high)
- But the **growth rate is decelerating**, particularly among secular/traditional families — Emuni's primary target market
- Net migration turning negative means fewer new families arriving, but also fewer foreign caregivers

### Key Takeaway
The market is NOT shrinking. A TFR of 2.85 means Israel continues to produce far more children per family than any other developed country. The secular fertility decline is real but slow — Emuni has a 10+ year runway before this becomes a headwind. The more immediate issue is that negative migration reduces both demand AND supply simultaneously.

## 6. Childcare Crisis & Shortage

### Scale of the Problem
- **114,000 toddlers** currently in childcare settings that operate illegally and without oversight (professional estimates)
- **1,000-1,500 illegal daycare facilities** operating nationwide, many in private homes
- In the Haredi community: only **7,000 of ~150,000 children under 3** are in supervised facilities (4.7%)
- Great shortage of carers with "no response given to the issue of manpower in the field"

### Jerusalem Daycare Tragedy (January 2026)
- Two infants died (Leah Goloventzitz, 4 months; Aharon Katz, 6 months) at an unlicensed Jerusalem daycare
- 53 babies and toddlers injured
- Cause: suspected heat exhaustion and dehydration from faulty heating system
- Triggered national outcry and legislative push for the Early Childhood Authority

### Systemic Failures
- 2021 supervision law only covers facilities with 7+ children — smaller home-based care is unregulated
- Enforcement budget slashed 76% (NIS 190M in 2023 to NIS 46M in 2026)
- Only 59 inspectors for the entire country
- An additional infant death at an unlicensed Bnei Brak daycare further intensified scrutiny

### Key Takeaway
Israel has a genuine childcare crisis — a combination of inadequate supply, dangerous unregulated facilities, and collapsing enforcement. This is a STRONG tailwind for Emuni: parents are desperate for trustworthy childcare options with transparency and accountability. A platform that provides verified profiles, reviews, and safety features directly addresses the trust deficit that the unlicensed daycare tragedies exposed.

## 8. Regulatory Landscape

### Israel-Specific Regulation

**Daycare Supervision Law (2021):**
- Extended reporting and oversight to private facilities with 7+ children
- Does NOT cover smaller home-based settings or individual nanny/babysitter arrangements
- Enforcement severely underfunded (see Section 6)

**Proposed Early Childhood Authority:**
- Would centralize all 0-3 oversight under Education Ministry
- If passed, could eventually extend to platform-mediated care — but this is speculative and likely years away
- Current focus is on physical daycare facilities, NOT marketplace platforms

**Employment Service Law 1959:**
- Cannot charge nannies/babysitters fees for finding work (existing Emuni design already complies — fees to parents only)

### Global Regulatory Trends

**EU Platform Work Directive (Directive 2024/2831):**
- Effective December 1, 2024; EU member states must implement by **December 2, 2026**
- Establishes **rebuttable presumption of employment** for platform workers where platform exercises "direction and control"
- Requires transparency on algorithmic management and decision-making
- **Not directly applicable to Israel**, but sets a global precedent
- Emuni's pure marketplace model (no control over nanny's work) is designed to stay outside employment classification

**ILO Standard-Setting:**
- International Labour Organization began formal standard-setting on platform work in 2025
- Aiming for international guidance/binding conventions on gig worker protections
- Timeline: years away from implementation

**US Developments:**
- New Mexico: first state to offer free childcare regardless of income
- Connecticut: $300M endowment for early care and education
- Federal: proposed CCDF rule changes in early 2026 (deregulatory direction)

### Key Takeaway
No immediate regulatory headwinds for Emuni's model. The Israeli government is focused on regulating physical daycare facilities (the unlicensed daycare crisis), not marketplace platforms. The EU Platform Work Directive doesn't apply to Israel, and Emuni's marketplace model (where nannies set their own rates, schedules, and methods) is specifically designed to avoid the "direction and control" triggers. The Employment Service Law prohibition on charging workers is already accounted for in Emuni's parent-pays model.
