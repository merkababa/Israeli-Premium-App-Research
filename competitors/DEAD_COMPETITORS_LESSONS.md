# Dead & Dying Vaad Bayit Apps: Autopsy Report

**Date: March 2026 | Purpose: Learn from the graveyard so we don't join it**

---

## Executive Summary

Seven vaad bayit apps have failed or are dying in Israel: **Hombi**, **Homenet**, **Shchenim**, **RunMaxi**, **Tarbut HaDiur**, **Neighbor**, and **Vaad Plus**. Despite spanning 10+ years of attempts, the combined active user base of ALL seven is likely under 3,000 buildings -- out of an estimated 150,000-250,000 residential buildings in Israel.

The failures share a devastating pattern: **no business model, no distribution, no full-time commitment, and a fundamental misunderstanding of who the customer is and what they actually need.** Even RunMaxi, which had backing from Israel's two largest banks (HaPoalim and Leumi) AND Innovation Authority funding, failed -- proving that money alone cannot crack this market.

The single most important lesson: **Distribution beats product. Every time.** Hombi built the best product in the market (4.8/5 stars) and has the worst growth. Ding/OXS, with a merely "good" product but Bar-Oz Insurance's 50-year distribution network, dominates with 120K households.

### 1. Hombi (הומבי) — Best Product, No Business

**Status: Zombie (still works, nobody running it)**

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Founded** | 2015, Jerusalem |
| **Founders** | Oron Ben-David (co-founder), Ovad Tzion (CEO & co-founder) |
| **Funding** | **$0 -- zero, entirely bootstrapped** |
| **Total downloads** | 73,000 (Google Play) |
| **Current downloads** | ~540/month and declining |
| **Ratings** | 4.80/5 (470 ratings Android), 4.5/5 (77 ratings iOS) |
| **Revenue model** | Free app + NIS 29.90/month for payment collection per building |
| **Tech** | Ionic hybrid (2015-era), package: `com.ionicframework.vaadapp212468` |
| **Android version** | 1.0.7 (after 10 years) |
| **iOS last update** | Sep 2024 (v5.6) |
| **Active buildings** | Claimed 13,000 Israel / 90,000 global; realistic: ~2,000-3,000 active |

#### What They Built

The most feature-complete free vaad bayit app on the market:
- Internal messaging (building-level)
- Voting and surveys for building decisions
- Professional directory (vetted contractors)
- Problem/fault reporting with photos
- Financial reports accessible to all residents
- Credit card payment for vaad fees (NIS 29.90/month extra)
- Task management and maintenance reminders
- Building maintenance module (added Sep 2024)
- Event room booking
- Document storage (protocols, minutes)
- Forums

Users genuinely loved it: "By far the most advanced and user friendly solution I have seen" (David Kaplan, 5/5). "All-in-One solution... first-class customer service" (doronp, 5/5).

#### How They Failed

**1. Fatal Business Model:**
Hombi charged a flat NIS 29.90/month per building for its only paid feature (payment collection). This means a 4-unit walk-up pays the same as a 200-unit luxury tower. Even at their claimed 13,000 Israeli buildings, if 20% paid: 2,600 x NIS 29.90 = NIS 77,740/month -- barely one developer's salary in Israel (avg tech: NIS 39,810/month).

Compare to Bllink's 1.5-3% commission model: a 20-unit building at NIS 200/unit generates NIS 60-120/month for Bllink vs. NIS 29.90 for Hombi.

**2. Side Project, Not a Company:**
Neither founder was ever full-time. Both were HP/HPE employees building on weekends. Evidence: Ionic Framework chosen for speed not quality; version 1.0.7 after 10 years; zero press coverage ever; zero fundraising.

**3. Founders Left:**
- **Ovad Tzion** (CEO) is now COO at DealHub.io, a well-funded ($60M+) B2B SaaS company. The Apple Developer account still lists his personal name.
- **Oron Ben-David** is virtually untraceable -- no LinkedIn, no press mentions, no subsequent ventures. Appears to have left tech entirely.

**4. No Distribution Strategy:**
Zero marketing in 10 years. No Facebook ads, no content marketing, no partnerships, no press outreach. Growth was purely organic word-of-mouth, which peaked and declined.

**5. Tried to Replace WhatsApp:**
Required ALL residents to download the app for it to work. In buildings with elderly residents and low tech literacy, this is an impossible adoption barrier.

#### Key Lesson

**A 4.8-star product with NIS 29.90/month revenue and zero distribution will always lose to a 3-star product with a real business model and distribution channel.** Product quality is necessary but nowhere near sufficient.

### 2. RunMaxi — Bank Backing + Government Funding = Still Failed

**Status: Pivoted to physical property management company (effectively dead as tech startup)**

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|

| **Original vision** | AI digital property management platform with WhatsApp/chatbot interface |
| **Product** | "Maxi" -- chatbot on WhatsApp, Facebook, Google Home, Siri, Alexa |
| **Investors** | **Bank HaPoalim + Bank Leumi** (Israel's two largest banks) |
| **Government backing** | **Israel Innovation Authority** (formerly Chief Innovation Scientist) |
| **Employees** | <25 |
| **Buildings managed** | ~30 (as of most recent data) |
| **Current website** | runmaxi.co.il redirects to PMC.im (a property management directory listing) |
| **Current team** | Moshe Zada (CEO), T'zion Zada (VP Operations), Racheli Pitusi (Community Relations) -- family-run |
| **Facebook rating** | 3.2/5 |

#### What They Built

RunMaxi was ambitious -- perhaps the most technically sophisticated attempt in the space:
- AI chatbot ("Maxi") available on WhatsApp, Facebook Messenger, Google Home, Siri, Alexa
- Digital platform for managing buildings, tracking residents, payments, maintenance
- Supplier order management (pricing, execution, verification, payment)
- Claimed 30-50% cost savings across managed units
- "Trusted by tens of thousands of tenants" (marketing claim from ~2019)

They even absorbed competitor **Neighbor** in October 2019 when that app shut down.

#### How They Failed

**1. Over-Engineered the Wrong Problem:**
Building an AI chatbot on 6+ platforms (WhatsApp, Facebook, Google Home, Siri, Alexa) before having product-market fit. A vaad bayit chair doesn't need to talk to Alexa about their building -- they need residents to pay on time.

**2. Money Without Market Understanding:**
Despite backing from Israel's two largest banks and Innovation Authority grants, RunMaxi had only ~30 buildings. Bank backing opened doors to meetings, not to building adoption. Vaad bayit chairs don't choose software because a bank recommends it -- they choose it because their neighbor uses it.

**3. Pivoted Away from Tech:**
The most damning signal: RunMaxi abandoned its own technology platform and became a traditional physical property management company. runmaxi.co.il now redirects to PMC.im, a directory listing. This means the founders themselves concluded that the technology couldn't sell.

**4. Family Business Trap:**
Current team is Moshe Zada (CEO), T'zion Zada (VP Ops) -- a family operation managing 30 buildings. This is a small property management business, not a tech startup. The pivot from "AI digital platform backed by major banks" to "family managing 30 buildings" represents total failure of the original vision.

**5. Absorbed a Dying Competitor:**
Acquiring Neighbor (which died Oct 2019) brought users from one failing product to another. Inheriting a dead app's users is not growth -- it's hospice care.

#### Key Lesson

**Money and institutional backing cannot buy adoption in a market where decisions are made by volunteer committees of 3-5 people who just want less hassle, not more technology.** RunMaxi proves that the vaad bayit market resists top-down technology imposition. Adoption happens bottom-up, one building at a time, through word-of-mouth and demonstrated value.

#### What They Built

A government-backed organization providing:

- Legal advice (Hebrew only)
- Engineering consultations
- Computer program for committee management
- Dual-authentication login (SMS to registered committee chair)
- Various guides on shared housing law and management

#### How They Failed

**1. Government Pace, Startup Market:**
Being a government-supervised non-profit means decisions move at bureaucratic speed. While Bllink and Ding iterate weekly, Tarbut HaDiur operates on government procurement timelines.

**2. Published an RFI to Replace Its Own Software (2025):**
The most damning signal: the organization publicly acknowledged its own software is inadequate and issued a Request for Information to find a replacement. When the organization that built the software says it needs replacing, the product is dead.

**3. Members-Only Model:**
You must be a paying member of the association to access the software. This creates an unnecessary paywall in a market where free and cheap alternatives exist.

**4. Hebrew-Only, No Mobile:**
Web-only interface, Hebrew only, dated UI. No mobile app. In 2026, this is not a competitive product.

**5. Tiny Reach:**
~1,600 users out of 150,000-250,000 buildings = 0.6-1.1% penetration after decades of existence. Government backing couldn't drive adoption.

#### Key Lesson

**Government-backed software with a captive audience of 1,600 still failed to achieve meaningful adoption.** Institutional credibility and legal expertise are not enough to overcome poor technology and distribution. Also: if even the creator wants to replace the software, it's time to let it go.

## Common Failure Patterns

Across all seven dead/dying competitors, five patterns repeat with devastating consistency:

### Pattern 1: No Business Model (or a Broken One)

| Company | Revenue Model | Why It Failed |
|---------|--------------|---------------|
| **Hombi** | NIS 29.90/month flat | Doesn't scale with building size. Max ~$22K/month at full penetration |
| **Homenet** | None | Hobby project, never monetized |
| **Shchenim** | CC processing fees | Lost payment processor in 2020 -- game over |
| **RunMaxi** | Unclear/pivoted | Over-invested in tech before finding revenue model |
| **Neighbor** | Unclear | Couldn't sustain independently, absorbed by RunMaxi |
| **Tarbut HaDiur** | Annual membership | Too low to invest in product improvement |
| **Vaad Plus** | Never launched | N/A |

**The pattern:** 5 out of 7 were either free or had revenue models that couldn't fund a single developer. The remaining 2 (RunMaxi, Tarbut HaDiur) had external funding but still couldn't build sustainable models.

### Pattern 2: No Full-Time Commitment

| Company | Team Commitment | Evidence |
|---------|----------------|---------|
| **Hombi** | Side project (HP employees) | v1.0.7 after 10 years, both founders left |
| **Homenet** | Solo hobby | One developer in Kfar Saba, Bootstrap 3 |
| **Shchenim** | Unknown (likely minimal) | Zero updates post-2020 |
| **RunMaxi** | Initially committed, then pivoted | Abandoned own technology |
| **Vaad Plus** | Zero commitment | Never launched |

**The pattern:** Building a marketplace product (two-sided: vaad chair + residents) as a side project is a death sentence. You need full-time commitment to solve the adoption chicken-and-egg problem.

### Pattern 3: No Distribution Strategy

| Company | Distribution Approach | Result |
|---------|----------------------|--------|
| **Hombi** | Organic only, zero marketing | 73K downloads in 10 years (7.3K/year) |
| **RunMaxi** | Bank referrals + AI hype | 30 buildings |
| **Neighbor** | Unknown | Dead |
| **Shchenim** | Unknown | Dead |
| **Homenet** | None | Zero evidence of adoption |
| **Tarbut HaDiur** | Government membership pipeline | 1,600 users in decades |
| **Vaad Plus** | None | Never launched |

**The pattern:** Compare to Ding/OXS which has 120K households via Bar-Oz Insurance's 50-year network, or Bllink with 65K apartments from Wix founder connections + active marketing. Distribution is the ONLY predictor of success in this market.

### Pattern 4: Tried to Replace WhatsApp Instead of Working Through It

| Company | Messaging Approach | Problem |
|---------|-------------------|---------|
| **Hombi** | Built internal messaging system | Requires all residents to download app |
| **RunMaxi** | Built chatbot on 6+ platforms | Over-engineered; vaad chairs don't use Alexa for building management |
| **Neighbor** | Built own communication layer | Another download barrier |
| **Shchenim** | Web-based messaging | Nobody went to a website to communicate |

**The pattern:** 80%+ of Israelis already use WhatsApp daily. Every building already has a WhatsApp group. Building a competing messaging system is fighting the strongest network effect in Israel.

### Pattern 5: Targeted Volunteer Vaad Chairs Without Understanding Their Constraints

| Constraint | Reality | What These Apps Assumed |
|-----------|---------|------------------------|
| **Budget** | Volunteers have no budget authority beyond basic fees | "They'll pay for software" |
| **Time** | Vaad service is unpaid, done after work hours | "They'll learn a new platform" |
| **Authority** | Must convince entire building to adopt | "If the chair likes it, adoption follows" |
| **Tech literacy** | Must serve 80-year-olds and 25-year-olds | "Everyone will download our app" |
| **Motivation** | Most chairs serve reluctantly for 1-2 years, then rotate | "They'll be long-term users" |

**The pattern:** These apps built for an idealized power user who doesn't exist. The real vaad chair is an exhausted volunteer who wants LESS complexity, not more.

## The Graveyard Pattern: Why Has No One Cracked This Market?

### The Structural Challenge

The vaad bayit market has a uniquely toxic combination of characteristics that kills startups:

**1. Two-Sided Adoption Problem:**
Unlike B2B SaaS (sell to one decision-maker), vaad bayit apps need BOTH the committee (3-5 people) AND the residents (20-200 people) to adopt. Getting 50%+ adoption in a building with diverse ages, tech literacy, and motivation is extraordinarily difficult.

**2. The Customer Has No Budget:**
The vaad chair is an unpaid volunteer. They cannot authorize significant spending without a building assembly vote. Getting 20-80 residents to vote "yes" on software spending when "WhatsApp is free" is nearly impossible.

**3. High Churn by Design:**
Vaad chairs typically rotate every 1-2 years. Your champion who adopted the platform leaves, and the new chair may not want it. You lose buildings not because of product failure but because of natural leadership turnover.

**4. Excel Is "Good Enough" for Most:**
A 12-unit building with NIS 200/month fees is genuinely manageable with a notebook or spreadsheet. The pain point isn't acute enough for buildings under ~20 units -- which is the majority of Israeli buildings.

**5. Professional Management Is the Alternative:**
Larger buildings (40+ units) increasingly hire professional management at NIS 50-120/apartment/month. These companies use enterprise tools (Build-App, DARIMPO). The volunteer vaad bayit app is squeezed between "WhatsApp is enough" (small buildings) and "we'll hire professionals" (large buildings).

**6. WhatsApp Inertia Is Enormous:**
Every building already has a WhatsApp group. It handles announcements, complaints, and informal voting. It requires zero downloads, zero onboarding, zero learning. You're not competing with other apps -- you're competing with the most entrenched communication platform in Israel.

### Why The Survivors Survived

Only two platforms are genuinely growing: Ding/OXS (120K households) and Bllink (65K apartments). What do they have that the dead apps didn't?

| Factor | Dead Apps | Ding/OXS | Bllink |
|--------|-----------|----------|--------|
| **Distribution** | None/organic only | Bar-Oz Insurance 50-year network | Wix founder network + active marketing |
| **Revenue model** | Free/flat fee/unclear | NIS 10/apt/month (scales with building size) | 1.5-3% commission (scales with payment volume) |
| **Full-time team** | Side projects/hobbies | Full company (OXS Fintech Ltd) | 11-50 employees, $1.6-3.5M raised |
| **Business model first** | Built product, hoped revenue follows | Revenue model designed before product | Revenue from day 1 (commission on payments) |
| **Payment focus** | Community/messaging features first | Payments + compliance | Payments-first |

**The survivors prove the formula: Payments first (immediate value) + Real distribution channel + Revenue model that scales + Full-time commitment.**

## Top 10 Lessons for Our Platform

### Lesson 1: Revenue Model Must Scale With Building Size

**What went wrong:** Hombi's flat NIS 29.90/month means a 200-unit luxury tower pays the same as a 4-unit walk-up. Maximum revenue per building is capped at a trivial amount regardless of value delivered.

**What we must do:** Our pricing must increase with the value we deliver. A percentage-based transaction fee (like Bllink's 1.5%) or per-apartment pricing (like Ding's NIS 10/apt) means bigger buildings = more revenue. Our NIS 49/month flat fee + 1.5% transaction fee is a hybrid that captures both small buildings (floor) and large buildings (percentage).

### Lesson 2: Distribution Strategy From Day One, Not Day 365

**What went wrong:** Hombi did zero marketing in 10 years. RunMaxi relied on bank referrals that didn't convert. Shchenim, Homenet, and Vaad Plus had no distribution at all.

**What we must do:** Launch with a distribution plan: Facebook groups (Israeli vaad bayit communities), WhatsApp referral incentives (first month free for every building you bring), content marketing (Hebrew blog about vaad bayit rights and tips), partnerships with insurance agents and accountants who serve building committees.

### Lesson 3: Work THROUGH WhatsApp, Never Against It

**What went wrong:** Every dead app tried to replace WhatsApp with its own messaging system. This requires ALL residents to download a new app -- an impossible adoption barrier.

**What we must do:** Residents should NEVER need to download our app. Payment links go INTO existing WhatsApp groups. Reminders arrive via WhatsApp. Financial reports are shareable links. Only the vaad chair needs the dashboard. Everyone else just clicks links in WhatsApp.

### Lesson 4: Full-Time Commitment or Don't Enter

**What went wrong:** Hombi was built by HP employees on weekends. Homenet is a solo hobby. These part-time efforts produced part-time results.

**What we must do:** This must be a full-time effort from day one. The two-sided adoption problem (committee + residents) requires constant iteration, support, and growth hacking. A marketplace product cannot be built in spare hours.

### Lesson 5: Payments First, Everything Else Second

**What went wrong:** Hombi built 12+ features (messaging, voting, task management, forums, etc.) but only NIS 29.90 from payments. RunMaxi built AI chatbots on 6 platforms but couldn't convert buildings.

**What we must do:** MVP = payments. Getting residents to pay vaad fees digitally (via WhatsApp link) is the core value proposition. Maintenance, voting, documents are v2+ features. If we nail payments, everything else is gravy.

### Lesson 6: Solve for the Exhausted Volunteer, Not the Power User

**What went wrong:** These apps built comprehensive management suites for an idealized vaad chair who wants to become a property management professional. Real vaad chairs are exhausted volunteers who want LESS work.

**What we must do:** Our value proposition is "your residents will pay without you chasing them." Not "become a professional building manager." Reduce the chair's work, don't increase it. Automate everything: reminders, receipts, reports, debt notifications.

### Lesson 7: Don't Build What You Can't Sustain

**What went wrong:** RunMaxi built on 6+ platforms (WhatsApp, Facebook, Google Home, Siri, Alexa) with AI before having product-market fit. Maintaining all those integrations with <25 employees is impossible.

**What we must do:** Build for web + WhatsApp first. Mobile app second. Each platform we support must carry its weight in users. No vanity integrations.

### Lesson 8: Plan for Chair Turnover

**What went wrong:** None of the dead apps addressed the fact that vaad chairs rotate every 1-2 years. When the champion leaves, the building often abandons the platform.

**What we must do:** Design for handoff: one-click transfer of chair role, automatic onboarding email for new chairs, make the platform valuable for RESIDENTS (not just chairs) so there's bottom-up pressure to keep it when chairs change.

### Lesson 9: Don't Absorb Dying Competitors (Build Your Own Moat)

**What went wrong:** RunMaxi absorbed Neighbor (2019). Neighbor's users went from one failing product to another. No value was created -- just hospice care.

**What we must do:** Focus on organic growth and making our own product irreplaceable. If a competitor dies, their users will find us naturally if we're better -- we don't need to acquire zombie user bases.

### Lesson 10: Validate Revenue Before Building Features

**What went wrong:** Every dead app built features before validating that anyone would pay. Hombi built 12+ features and charges NIS 29.90. RunMaxi built AI chatbots and manages 30 buildings.

**What we must do:** Before building a single feature beyond payments, validate willingness to pay. Run a landing page, collect signups, charge NIS 49/month from the first building. If 10 buildings pay without us building maintenance/voting/documents, we have product-market fit on payments alone. Then layer features.

## The Meta-Lesson: This Market Is Hard, But Not Impossible

The graveyard of vaad bayit apps tells a consistent story: **the market is real (150,000-250,000 buildings), the pain is real (chasing payments, transparency, communication), but the solution requires a very specific combination of elements that no dead competitor had:**

1. **Payment-first value prop** (immediate, measurable ROI)
2. **WhatsApp-native delivery** (zero adoption barrier for residents)
3. **Real distribution channel** (not just "build it and they will come")
4. **Revenue model that scales** (percentage, not flat fee)
5. **Full-time commitment** (marketplace products die as side projects)
6. **Designed for real users** (exhausted volunteers, not power users)

Ding/OXS has elements 1, 3, 4, 5, and 6 -- which is why they lead with 120K households.
Bllink has elements 1, 3, 4, 5 -- which is why they're #2 with 65K apartments.
**No one has element 2 (WhatsApp-native).** That is our opening.

The dead apps had, at most, 1-2 of these elements. That's why they're dead.
