## 1. Platform vs Employer Classification

### 1.1 The Core Legal Risk

The single biggest legal risk for a nanny matching platform in Israel is being classified as the **employer** of the nannies rather than a neutral marketplace. If classified as an employer, the platform would owe every nanny:

- Pension contributions (6.5% employer share)
- Severance pay (8.33% of salary)
- Vacation days, sick leave, recuperation pay (דמי הבראה)
- Social security (Bituach Leumi) contributions
- Compliance with minimum wage laws
- Protection against unfair dismissal

This could make the business model unviable.

### 1.2 Key Israeli Laws Governing Classification

#### Employment of Workers by Manpower Contractors Law (חוק העסקת עובדים על ידי קבלני כוח אדם), 1996

- A **manpower contractor** (קבלן כוח אדם) is an entity that supplies workers to perform work that constitutes part of the regular activities of a client business.
- Operating as a manpower contractor **requires a license** from the Ministry of Labor.
- Temporary staffing placements are limited to **9 months**, after which the worker must be directly hired by the client or terminated.
- A manpower contractor is considered the employer and must provide all statutory employee rights.

**Risk for nanny platform:** If the platform is deemed to be supplying nannies as a manpower contractor, it must obtain a license and bear all employer obligations.

#### Employment Service Law (חוק שירות התעסוקה), 1959 — Chapter 4: Private Employment Bureaus

- A **private employment bureau** (לשכה פרטית) that provides job placement/matching services must hold a valid license.
- Private employment bureaus are **prohibited from charging job seekers** any fees — they can only charge employers.
- Licensing takes approximately 3 months and requires demonstrating knowledge of relevant regulations, appropriate facilities, clean criminal record, and no enforcement actions in the prior 3 years.

**Risk for nanny platform:** If the platform charges nannies fees or is seen as a placement agency, it may need a private employment bureau license.

#### Recruitment Agency vs. Marketplace — The Critical Distinction

Israeli law distinguishes between:

| Category | Definition | License Required? | Employer Obligations? |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|----------------------|
| **Manpower Contractor** (קבלן כוח אדם) | Employs workers and supplies them to clients | Yes — Ministry of Labor license | Yes — full employer |
| **Private Employment Bureau** (לשכה פרטית) | Matches job seekers with employers (placement) | Yes — Employment Service Law license | No — but cannot charge job seekers |
| **Broker/Intermediary** (מתווך) | Facilitates connections between parties | Depends on activity | No — if truly neutral |
| **Classifieds/Marketplace Platform** | Provides listing space, no active matching | No specific license | No |

### 1.3 The Israeli "Control Test" for Employment Classification

Israeli labor courts use a multi-factor test that examines the **economic reality** of the relationship, not the contractual label. The key factors are:

1. **Integration test**: Is the worker's function an integral and regular part of the platform's business?
2. **Control and supervision**: Does the platform control how, when, and where work is performed?
3. **Financial dependence**: Is the worker financially dependent on the platform?
4. **Economic risk**: Does the worker bear their own business risk, or does the platform absorb it?
5. **Tools and equipment**: Does the platform supply the means to work?
6. **Exclusivity**: Does the worker serve multiple clients independently?
7. **Subjective intention**: Did the parties intend an employer-employee relationship?

**Critical insight:** Israeli courts consistently prioritize substance over form. A contract stating "independent contractor" will be disregarded if the actual working relationship looks like employment.

### 1.4 How to Structure the Platform to AVOID Employer Classification

Based on the Wolt ruling analysis and Israeli legal framework, the platform should:

**DO:**
- Allow nannies to set their own rates
- Allow nannies to accept or reject any job freely, with no penalties
- Let families and nannies negotiate terms directly
- Allow nannies to work through multiple platforms simultaneously
- Let nannies choose their own schedule with zero platform interference
- Position as a "bulletin board" where families post needs and nannies respond
- Make the contractual relationship explicitly between family and nanny

**DO NOT:**
- Set or control nanny pricing/rates
- Dispatch or assign nannies to families algorithmically
- Penalize nannies for declining jobs or being unavailable
- Rate/score nannies in ways that affect their visibility or access
- Require uniforms, scripts, or specific work methods
- Process payments in a way that makes the platform the payer
- Provide training that implies an employment relationship
- Make nannies financially dependent on the platform as their primary income source

## 2. Wolt Israel Legal Precedent

### 2.1 Case Summary

**Case:** Class Action 35327-08-20, Khazanovitch v. Wolt Enterprises Israel Ltd.

**Court:** Tel Aviv Regional Labor Court (initial ruling August 2022)

**Ruling:** The court approved a class action and ruled that **Wolt is the employer of record** for all its thousands of couriers in Israel, obligating it to pay all statutory employee benefits including pension, vacation, sick leave, severance, and recuperation pay.

### 2.2 What Wolt Argued (and Lost)

Wolt claimed it was merely an **intermediary platform** (מתווך) connecting restaurants with independent courier contractors. The court **rejected this entirely**, finding:

1. **The app was a work tool, not a marketplace**: The app simultaneously located available couriers near restaurants and dispatched them — this was equivalent to classic employer instructions, not neutral matching.

2. **Wolt controlled pricing**: Wolt unilaterally set courier pay rates and had the power to change them without negotiation — a hallmark of employer control.

3. **Real-time supervision**: Through the app, Wolt could supervise, monitor, and communicate with couriers in real time, equivalent to workplace supervision.

4. **No independent business**: Couriers did not act independently — they were responsible only to Wolt, not to the restaurant or customer.

5. **Core business dependence**: The court stated **"Wolt has no business without the couriers"** — couriers were integral to Wolt's core business, not peripheral service providers.

6. **Customer ratings as control**: The feedback/rating system constituted a form of employer supervision and quality control.

### 2.3 Appeal Status (as of March 2026)

- Wolt appealed to the **National Labor Court** (בית הדין הארצי לעבודה).
- An appeal hearing was held in June 2023 before President Judge Varda Wirth-Livne.
- Multiple delays occurred due to the war. The Attorney General was expected to submit her position by mid-March 2024.
- The court proposed **mediation** (גישור) between the parties.
- As of the latest available information, the National Labor Court had not yet issued a final ruling on the appeal.

**Note:** In contrast, Finland's labor court ruled that Wolt couriers are **independent contractors** — but the Finnish test uses different criteria. The Israeli test is more protective of workers.

### 2.4 February 2022 Interministerial Committee

In February 2022, Israel's Minister of Economy and Industry appointed an **interministerial team** to examine the implications of the platform economy and its regulation. As of the court's ruling, no recommendations had been published and no legislation had been formulated — meaning the platform economy operates in a **regulatory gray zone** where court decisions set the rules.

### 2.5 Specific Mistakes Wolt Made That We Must Avoid

| Wolt's Mistake | Lesson for Nanny Platform |
|----------------|---------------------------|
| Set courier pay rates unilaterally | Let nannies set their own rates; families negotiate directly |
| Algorithmically dispatched couriers to jobs | Let nannies browse and choose jobs themselves |
| Used app for real-time monitoring/supervision | Platform should only facilitate initial connection, not monitor work |
| Penalized couriers for declining orders | No penalties for declining, no availability requirements |
| Controlled the entire transaction flow | Payment should flow directly family→nanny, platform takes a transparent fee |
| Called itself "intermediary" but acted as employer | Actually be an intermediary — not just call yourself one |
| Made couriers dependent (no other clients) | Encourage multi-platform use, no exclusivity |

## 3. Israeli Childcare Regulations

### 3.1 Daycare Supervision Law (חוק הפיקוח על מעונות יום לפעוטות), 2018

This law regulates **daycare centers** (מעונות יום) for toddlers and requires:

- **Operating license** for facilities caring for 7+ toddlers
- Criminal background checks for all staff, managers, owners, and anyone in direct contact with children
- First aid and safety training for educational-care staff
- Compliance with facility safety standards, health/nutrition standards, emergency procedures
- Staff-to-child ratio requirements

**Key question: Does this apply to a matching platform?**

**Analysis:** The Daycare Supervision Law applies to **institutions/facilities providing care**, not to platforms that connect families with individual caregivers. A nanny matching platform does not operate a daycare facility — it connects families with nannies who provide care in the family's home. Therefore, the platform itself likely does **not need a daycare license**.

However, this does not mean the platform has zero regulatory obligations related to childcare safety.

### 3.2 Law for Prevention of Employment of Sex Offenders in Certain Institutions (חוק למניעת העסקה של עברייני מין במוסדות מסוימים), 2001

This is a **critical law** for the nanny platform:

- **Prohibits** employment of adults convicted of sexual offenses in institutions serving children, people with disabilities, and vulnerable populations.
- The restriction applies for up to **20 years** after conviction (or 20 years after release from prison).
- Employers **must obtain police clearance** (אישור משטרה) confirming no sex offense convictions before hiring anyone who will work with children.
- An appeals committee can grant exemptions in limited circumstances.

**Application to matching platform:**

Even if the platform is not the "employer," there is a strong argument that:
- The platform has a **duty of care** to verify that nannies listed on the platform have clean criminal records regarding sex offenses.
- Failure to conduct background checks could expose the platform to **tort liability** for negligent screening if a nanny with a sex offense record harms a child.
- This is both a legal and business necessity — families will expect and demand this.

**Recommendation:** Require all nannies to provide police clearance (אישור העדר עבירות מין) as a condition of listing on the platform. Store verification status, not the raw criminal record data.

### 3.3 Criminal Information and Rehabilitation Law (חוק המידע הפלילי ותקנת השבים), 2019

This law governs who can access criminal record information and for what purposes. Recent amendments (effective 2022+) expanded the authority to check criminal backgrounds for those working with children to include:

- Teaching assistants (סייעות)
- Tutorial support staff
- Camp counselors
- Maintenance workers in educational settings

**Implication:** The trend in Israeli law is expanding — not narrowing — background check requirements for anyone working with children. A platform that facilitates access to children should proactively implement thorough screening.

### 3.4 General Criminal Background Checks

Outside the specific sex offender law, **general criminal record checks for employment purposes are prohibited in Israel**, except for government security positions and other specifically designated roles. This means:

- The platform **can and should** require sex offense clearance (specifically authorized by law)
- The platform **cannot** require a full criminal record check for general crimes
- The platform should clearly communicate to nannies that only the sex offense check is required and that it is a legal requirement

## 4. Privacy and Data Protection

### 4.1 Israel's Privacy Protection Law (חוק הגנת הפרטיות), 1981

This is the primary data protection law in Israel, significantly updated by **Amendment 13** (adopted August 2024, effective August 14, 2025).

### 4.2 Amendment 13 — Key Requirements for the Platform

Amendment 13 aligns Israeli law more closely with the GDPR and introduces:

#### Data Collection and Processing
- **Purpose limitation**: Data must be collected for a specific, defined purpose and not used beyond that purpose.
- **Data minimization**: Collect only the minimum data necessary for the platform's operation.
- **Legal basis**: Processing requires either consent, contractual necessity, legal obligation, or legitimate interest.

#### Database Registration
- **Mandatory registration** with the Privacy Protection Authority (PPA) if the platform's primary purpose is collecting personal data for transfer to others as a business activity AND the database contains data on more than 10,000 individuals.
- **Notification requirement**: If processing sensitive data on more than 100,000 individuals, must notify PPA of identity and contact details, even without registration.
- A nanny matching platform **likely meets registration thresholds** once it scales, as its core function is collecting and sharing personal data between parties.

#### Privacy Protection Officer
- Must appoint a **Privacy Protection Officer** (ממונה על הגנת הפרטיות) if:
  - Processing sensitive data at scale
  - Engaging in systematic monitoring
  - Operating as a data broker
- The officer must have legal expertise, IT/cybersecurity knowledge, and report directly to senior management.

#### Data Security
- Implement **reasonable and appropriate measures** to safeguard personal data:
  - Encryption at rest and in transit
  - Strict access control
  - Routine security audits and vulnerability assessments
  - Documentation of security protocols
- Large sensitive databases must undergo **risk assessments and penetration testing every 18 months**.

#### Breach Notification
- Amendment 13 introduces **mandatory breach notification** to the PPA and affected individuals in case of data breaches involving personal data.

### 4.3 Data Types the Platform Will Handle and Their Sensitivity

| Data Category | Sensitivity Level | Special Requirements |
|--------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Nanny personal details (name, phone, address) | Standard personal data | Standard protection |
| Family contact information | Standard personal data | Standard protection |
| Children's names, ages, special needs | **Sensitive** — involves minors | Parental consent required; heightened protection |
| Background check results | **Highly sensitive** — criminal record data | Store only pass/fail status, not raw data; legal basis required |
| Location data | Sensitive if tracked in real-time | Avoid real-time tracking (also avoids employer classification) |
| Payment/financial data | Sensitive | PCI-DSS compliance if processing cards; prefer third-party payment processor |
| Reviews/ratings | Standard | Subject to defamation law if published |
| Health information (allergies, medical needs of children) | **Sensitive** — medical data | Explicit consent; purpose limitation |

### 4.4 Children's Data Protection

- Israel currently has **no specific children's data protection law** (unlike COPPA in the US).
- However, since children cannot provide legal consent, **parental/guardian consent** is required for processing any data about children.
- The PPA published guidance in January 2022 on safeguarding children's privacy in online learning environments — the principles apply broadly.
- **Recommendation:** Minimize children's data. Collect only what families choose to share (age ranges, special needs) and never create profiles of individual children.

### 4.5 Background Check Data Storage

- Store only the **verification status** (verified/not verified/expired) and the **date of verification**.
- Do NOT store the actual police clearance certificate or criminal record data.
- Implement automatic **expiration/re-verification** cycles (recommended: annual re-check).
- Restrict access to verification data to authorized personnel only.

## 5. Liability and Insurance

### 5.1 Tort Liability Framework

Israel's tort law is governed by the **Civil Wrongs Ordinance (New Version), 1968** (פקודת הנזיקין). Key principles:

#### Negligence (Section 35-36)
A person is negligent when they fail to act as a **reasonable and prudent person** would. The duty of care exists toward all persons a reasonable person ought to have foreseen might be affected.

For a nanny matching platform, potential negligence claims include:

1. **Negligent screening**: Failing to adequately vet nannies before listing them
2. **Negligent matching**: Matching a nanny known to be unsuitable for a specific situation
3. **Failure to warn**: Not warning families about known risks
4. **Negligent retention**: Keeping a nanny on the platform after receiving complaints

#### Negligent Selection of Contractor
Under Israeli tort law, there is liability for **negligence in the selection of a contractor**. Even if the nanny is an independent contractor (not an employee), the platform could be liable for negligently selecting/approving them.

#### Joint Tortfeasor Liability
Under Israeli law, a person who collaborates in the commission of a tort — **even if participation is merely indirect** — can be considered as having jointly caused the tort. This means a platform could share liability with a negligent nanny.

### 5.2 Platform Liability Precedents

While no Israeli court has specifically ruled on nanny matching platform liability, relevant principles emerge from:

- **The Wolt case**: Established that platforms exercising control are employers, not intermediaries — increasing liability exposure.
- **The Agoda Supreme Court ruling (2024)**: Israeli Consumer Protection Law applies to foreign platforms targeting Israeli consumers, and choice-of-law clauses in terms of service are unenforceable. This means Israeli law will apply regardless of where the platform is incorporated.
- **Class action exposure**: Israel has a robust class action framework, and consumer protection class actions are the most common type. Platforms are increasingly targeted.

### 5.3 Limitation of Liability in Terms of Service

Israeli law significantly limits the enforceability of liability disclaimers:

#### Standard Contracts Law (חוק החוזים האחידים), 1982
- Platform Terms of Service are considered **standard form contracts** (חוזים אחידים).
- **Unfair terms** (תנאים מקפחים) — those granting the platform an unreasonable advantage — can be voided by courts.
- Broad liability exclusions (e.g., "platform is not liable for any harm whatsoever") are likely to be deemed unfair and unenforceable.
- The Standard Contracts Tribunal can proactively review and strike unfair terms.

#### What WILL and WON'T Hold Up

| Clause Type | Likely Enforceable? | Notes |
|------------|-------------------|-------|
| "We are a matching platform, not an employer" | Partially — depends on actual behavior | Courts look at substance, not labels |
| "The contract is between family and nanny" | Yes — if the platform truly stays out of the relationship | Strengthened if platform doesn't control work terms |
| "We don't guarantee nanny quality" | Partially — cannot disclaim duty of care for screening | Must still exercise reasonable screening |
| "We are not liable for acts of nannies" | Limited — cannot disclaim negligent selection | May survive for nanny's independent negligence |
| "Disputes subject to arbitration" | Yes — generally enforceable in Israel | But consumer class actions may override |
| "Platform not liable for any damages" | No — overly broad, likely struck as unfair term | Must be reasonable and specific |

### 5.4 Recommended Insurance

| Insurance Type | Purpose | Priority |
|---------------|---------|----------|
| **Professional Liability (E&O)** | Covers claims of negligent screening, matching errors | Essential |
| **General Liability** | Covers third-party bodily injury and property damage claims | Essential |
| **Cyber Liability** | Covers data breaches and privacy violations | Essential (given sensitive data) |
| **Directors & Officers (D&O)** | Protects company leadership from personal liability | Important |
| **Employment Practices Liability (EPLI)** | Covers claims of misclassification, wrongful termination | Important — especially given Wolt precedent |

**Recommendation:** Also consider requiring nannies to carry their own **personal liability insurance** as a condition of platform listing, or offering it as an optional add-on service through the platform (this exists in the Israeli insurance market).

## 6. Recommended Legal Structure

### 6.1 The Safest Model: "Pure Marketplace" (שוק דיגיטלי)

Structure the platform as a **pure digital marketplace/bulletin board** where:

1. **Families post needs** (looking for nanny, X hours, Y area, Z requirements)
2. **Nannies browse and respond** to listings that interest them
3. **Families and nannies communicate directly** through the platform
4. **Terms are negotiated directly** between family and nanny (rate, schedule, duties)
5. **The platform charges a subscription or listing fee** — NOT a percentage of wages
6. **The employment contract is between family and nanny** — the platform is not a party

This is essentially the **Yad2 model** applied to childcare — the platform is a classifieds service, not a service provider.

### 6.2 Revenue Model Implications

| Revenue Model | Legal Risk Level | Notes |
|--------------|-----------------|-------|
| **Subscription (monthly fee for families/nannies)** | Lowest risk | No connection to individual transactions; clearly a platform fee |
| **Listing fee (per posting)** | Low risk | Similar to classifieds; no per-transaction involvement |
| **Transaction fee (% of each booking)** | Minimal risk | Fee structure is legally irrelevant to worker classification — only the 9 control factors from the Wolt ruling matter. No Israeli case law where a platform's fee structure itself led to employer classification. |
| **Commission on wages** | Highest risk | Directly taking a cut of worker pay suggests employer role |

**Note on Employment Service Law constraint:** Private employment bureaus are **prohibited from charging job seekers any fees**. If the platform is classified as a private employment bureau, it cannot charge nannies. To avoid this classification entirely, the platform should position itself as a technology company providing listing services — not as a job placement agency.

### 6.3 Three-Party Contract Structure

**Recommended contractual framework:**

#### Contract 1: Platform ↔ Family (Terms of Service)
- Family acknowledges they are the employer of the nanny
- Family assumes all employer obligations under Israeli labor law
- Platform provides matching technology only
- Platform disclaims employer status explicitly
- Family agrees to conduct due diligence beyond platform screening
- Dispute resolution clause (arbitration preferred)

#### Contract 2: Platform ↔ Nanny (Service Provider Agreement)
- Nanny acknowledges independent status
- Nanny sets own rates, schedule, and availability
- Nanny is free to work through other platforms and directly
- Nanny consents to background check verification
- Nanny represents accuracy of qualifications and experience
- No exclusivity, no non-compete

#### Contract 3: Family ↔ Nanny (Employment Agreement — facilitated but NOT created by platform)
- The platform provides a **template** employment agreement families can use
- The template includes all Israeli labor law requirements (social benefits, vacation, sick leave, etc.)
- The family and nanny are the contracting parties — the platform is NOT a party
- This helps families comply with the law while keeping the platform out of the employer relationship

### 6.4 Operational Safeguards

To ensure the "pure marketplace" classification holds under judicial scrutiny:

1. **No algorithmic assignment**: Nannies choose their own engagements — the platform does not assign or dispatch.
2. **No price control**: Nannies set their own rates. The platform may display market rate ranges for informational purposes only.
3. **No quality scoring that affects access**: Reviews are informational only — not used to restrict platform access or visibility algorithmically.
4. **No penalties**: No penalties for declining jobs, being unavailable, or ending engagements.
5. **No real-time monitoring**: The platform does not track nanny location or activities during work.
6. **No payment processing through platform**: Families pay nannies directly. If the platform facilitates payment, it should use a third-party payment processor and make clear it is a convenience feature, not wage payment.
7. **Multi-platform encouraged**: Terms should explicitly state nannies may use competing platforms.

### 6.5 Company Registration

- Register as an Israeli **Ltd. company** (חברה בע"מ).
- Do NOT register as a manpower contractor or employment bureau unless specifically advised by counsel.
- Business description should emphasize "technology platform for connecting families with childcare providers" — not "childcare service provider."

## 7. Actionable Compliance Checklist

### Pre-Launch Legal Requirements

- [ ] Register company as Israeli Ltd. (חברה בע"מ)
- [ ] Consult with Israeli labor law attorney specializing in platform economy
- [ ] Draft Terms of Service reviewed against Standard Contracts Law
- [ ] Draft Nanny Service Provider Agreement
- [ ] Draft Family Terms of Use
- [ ] Create template Family-Nanny Employment Agreement
- [ ] Register database with Privacy Protection Authority (PPA) if applicable
- [ ] Appoint Privacy Protection Officer (if processing sensitive data at scale)
- [ ] Implement data security measures per Protection of Privacy Regulations (Data Security)
- [ ] Obtain Professional Liability (E&O) insurance
- [ ] Obtain General Liability insurance
- [ ] Obtain Cyber Liability insurance
- [ ] Build background check verification flow (sex offense clearance)
- [ ] Create privacy policy compliant with Amendment 13

### Ongoing Compliance

- [ ] Annual re-verification of nanny background checks
- [ ] Periodic review of Terms of Service by counsel (especially after new court rulings)
- [ ] Monitor Wolt National Labor Court appeal outcome — may require structural changes
- [ ] Conduct penetration testing every 18 months (if handling large sensitive database)
- [ ] Maintain data processing records
- [ ] Monitor PPA guidance and enforcement actions
- [ ] Track legislative developments (interministerial committee on platform economy)
- [ ] Regularly audit platform features to ensure they don't cross into employer behavior

### Red Lines — NEVER Do These

- Never set or control nanny pay rates
- Never algorithmically dispatch/assign nannies to families
- Never penalize nannies for declining or being unavailable
- Never monitor nannies during work sessions
- Never require exclusivity
- Never store raw criminal record data (only pass/fail status)
- Never collect children's data beyond what parents explicitly provide
- Never use sweeping liability disclaimers that would be struck as unfair terms
